\(\begin{bmatrix} 4&2\\ 2&4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}-1\\1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}-2\\2\end{bmatrix}\)

and notice that \(2\begin{bmatrix}-1\\1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}-2\\2\end{bmatrix}.\) Yes the vector is an eigenvector, and it is associated to the eigenvalue \(\lambda=2\).

8.

Consider the matrix \(A-\lambda I=A-3I\):

\(\begin{bmatrix}-2&2&2\\ 3&-5&1\\ 0&1&-2\end{bmatrix}\sim \begin{bmatrix}1&0&-3\\ 0&1&-2\\ 0&0&0\end{bmatrix}\)

The equation \((A-\lambda I){\bf x}={\bf 0}\) has a non-trivial solution. So, \(\lambda=3\) is an eigenvector. The general solution to the homogeneous equation is:

\(x_3\begin{bmatrix}3\\2\\1\end{bmatrix}\). Pick any non-zero value for \(x_3\) to obtain an eigenvector. Say \(x_3=2\), then \(\begin{bmatrix}6\\4\\2\end{bmatrix}\) is an eigenvector.

10.

\(A-\lambda I=\begin{bmatrix}6&-9\\4&-6\end{bmatrix}\sim \begin{bmatrix}1 & -\frac{9}{6}\\ 0&0\end{bmatrix}\)

An eigenvector is \(\begin{bmatrix}9\\6\end{bmatrix}\) which forms a basis for the eigenspace corresponding to \(\lambda=4\).

The general solution to the equation \((A-\lambda I){\bf x}={\bf 0}\) is

\[{\bf x}=x_3\begin{bmatrix}2\\3\\1\\0\end{bmatrix}+x_4\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}.\] A basis for the eigenspace is \[\left\{\begin{bmatrix}2\\3\\1\\0\end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}\right\}.\]
