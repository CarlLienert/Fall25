6.

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -6 & 4 & 0 & -1\\ 0 & 2 & -7 & 0 & 4\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 & -3\\ 0 & 0 & 3 & 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix}\]

First to get all zeros below the main diagonal we need to eliminate the 3 in the bottom row.

\(-3 \cdot R3 + R4 \rightarrow R4\):

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -6 & 4 & 0 & -1\\ 0 & 2 & -7 & 0 & 4\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 & -3\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -5 & 15 \end{bmatrix}\]

The next main goal is to make all the entries above the main diagonal 0. This would start with eliminating the 2 in the third row. The easiest way to do this is to first change the \(-5\) to a \(1\). So, the next step is to do that.

\(-\frac{1}{5}\cdot R4 \rightarrow R4\):

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -6 & 4 & 0 & -1\\ 0 & 2 & -7 & 0 & 4\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 & -3\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\]

(The third step would be to eliminate the 2 with \(-2 \cdot R4 + R3 \rightarrow R3\).)

This is the way humans do this process. Computers do it a little differently in order to minimize round off error.
