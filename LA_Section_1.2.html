12. and 18.

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -7 & 0 & 6 & 5\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & -3\\ -1 & 7 & -4 & 2 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\]

First, swap rows 2 and 3:

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -7 & 0 & 6 & 5\\ -1 & 7 & -4 & 2 & 7\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -7 & 0 & 6 & 5\\ 0 & 0 & -4 & 8 & 12\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -7 & 0 & 6 & 5\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & -3\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[\begin{bmatrix} \large{\bf{ 1}} & -7 & 0 & 6 & 5\\ 0 & 0 & \large{\bf{ 1}} & -2 & -3\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

The pivot columns are the 1st and 3rd columns. The free variables are \(x_2\), and \(x_3\).

From the first row we have:

\[\begin{align*} x_1-7x_2+6x_4&=5\\ x_1&=7x_2-6x_4+5 \end{align*}\]

From the second row we have:

\[\begin{align*} x_3-2x_4&=-3\\ x_3&=2x_4-3 \end{align*}\]

The general solution is:

\[\begin{cases} x_1=7x_2-6x_4+5\\ x_2 \ \ \textrm{is \ free}\\ x_3=2x_4-3\\ x_4 \ \ \textrm{is \ free} \end{cases}\]

The equation corresponding to the first row of the original matrix is: \[x_1-7x_2+0x_3+6x_4=5\]

We substitute the expressions for \(x_1\) and \(x_3\). The values for \(x_2\) and \(x_4\), the free variables, can be anything.

\[\begin{align*} x_1-7x_2+0x_3+6x_4&\overset{?}{=}5\\ (7x_2-6x_4+5)-7x_2+0\cdot(2x_4-3)+6x_4&\overset{?}{=}5\\ 7x_2-6x_4+5-7x_2+6x_4\overset{?}{=}5\\ 5\overset{\checkmark}{=}5 \end{align*}\]

The equations corresponding to rows 2 and 3 are similar.

24.

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 2\\ 3 & h & k \end{bmatrix}\]

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 2\\ 0 & h-9 & k-6 \end{bmatrix}\]

If \(h=9\) and \(k=6\) the bottom row is a zero row. In this case there is one free variable and the system is consistent with infinitely many solutions.

If \(h=9\) but \(k \neq 6\) there is a leading entry in the last column and so the system is inconsistent.

If \(h \neq 9\) the first and second columns are both pivot columns (no free variables) and the system is consistent regardless of the value of \(k\) and the solution is unique.

41. Let \(m\) be the number of equations. Let \(n\) be the number of unknowns. In an underdetermined systems \(m<n\). The coefficient matrix is a \(m \times n\) matrix. There can be at most \(m\) pivot columns, so there are at least \(n-m>0\) free variables. So, if the system is consistent there are infinitely many solutions due to the presence of free variables.

43. Yes, the reduced echelon form of the augmented matrix will have zero row(s). One way to build an example is make up two equations that have the same solutions. Say I want \(x_1=1\) and \(x_2=2\). Two equations with this solutions are: \(3x_1+4x_2=11\), and \(5x_1+6x_2=17\). (I plugged in the solution to see what the constant on the right side needs to be.) We need one more equation. It needs to be “redundant.” For example, let’s add the two equations we already have: \(8x_1+10x_2=28\). The overdetermined, consistent system is:

\[\begin{align*} 3x_1+4x_2&=11\\ 5x_1+6x_2&=17\\ 8x_1+10x_2&=28. \end{align*}\]

The corresponding reduced echelon augmented matrix is:

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1& 0 & 1\\ 0& 1 & 2\\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.\]
