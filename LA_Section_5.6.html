2.

If \[{\bf x}_0=a_1\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\-3\end{bmatrix}+a_2\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\-5\end{bmatrix}+a_3\begin{bmatrix}-3\\-3\\7\end{bmatrix}\] then \[\begin{align*} {\bf x}_k&=A^k{\bf x}_0\\ &= a_1A^k\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\-3\end{bmatrix}+a_2A^k\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\-5\end{bmatrix}+a_3A^k\begin{bmatrix}-3\\-3\\7\end{bmatrix}\\ &= a_1(3)^k\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\-3\end{bmatrix}+a_2\left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^k\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\-5\end{bmatrix}+a_3\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^k\begin{bmatrix}-3\\-3\\7\end{bmatrix}\end{align*}\]

As \(k \rightarrow \infty\), \({\bf x}_k \rightarrow a_1(3)^k\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\-3\end{bmatrix}.\)

That is long-term values of \({\bf x}_k\) are close to the direction given by \(\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\-3\end{bmatrix}.\)

Solving the system at the beginning of the problem give: \(a_1=2\), \(a_2=1\), and \(a_3=2\).

4.

\[A=\begin{bmatrix}0.5&0.4\\ -0.125& 1.1\end{bmatrix}\] has characteristic equation \[\left(\frac{1}{2}-\lambda\right)\left(\frac{11}{10}-\lambda\right)+\frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{2}{5}=\lambda^2-\frac{16}{10}\lambda+\frac{12}{20}.\] Solve:

\[\begin{align*} 0&=\lambda^2-\frac{8}{5}\lambda+\frac{3}{5}\\ 0&=5\lambda^2-8\lambda+3\\ &=(5\lambda-3)(\lambda-1) \end{align*}\]

The eigenvalues are \(\lambda=\frac{3}{5}, 1\).

For \(\lambda=1\) and eigenvector is \(\begin{bmatrix}4\\5\end{bmatrix}\)

The general solution for \({\bf x}_k\) is

\(a_1\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^k+a_2(1)^k\begin{bmatrix}4\\5\end{bmatrix}\)

Thus the long term ratio of owls to rats is \(4:5\). (4 owls for each 5 rats). Notice this is a very special case: one of the eigenvalues is \(\lambda=1\). If something changes, this eigenvalue will also change. If \(\lambda\) increases to larger than 1, then population will grow (unbounded according to the model). If the \(\lambda\) decreases to less than 1, both populations will decline.

6.

\[\displaystyle A=\begin{bmatrix}\frac{2}{5} & \frac{3}{10}\\ -p & \frac{6}{5}\end{bmatrix}\] has characteristic polynomial

\[\left(\frac{2}{5}-\lambda\right)\left(\frac{6}{5}-\lambda\right)+\frac{3p}{10}=\lambda^2-\frac{8}{5}\lambda+\frac{3p}{10}+\frac{12}{25}.\] The equation \[\lambda^2-\frac{8}{5}\lambda+\frac{3p}{10}+\frac{12}{25}=0\] is equivalent to the equation (clearing denominators) \[50\lambda^2-80\lambda+15p+24=0.\]

Roots are given by:

\[\begin{align*} \lambda&=\frac{80 \pm \sqrt{80^2-200(15p+24)}}{100}\\ &=\frac{80\pm \sqrt{1600-3000p}}{100}\\ &=\frac{80\pm 10\sqrt{16-30p}}{100}\\ &=\frac{8\pm\sqrt{16-30p}}{10}\end{align*}\]

If \(p=\frac{1}{2}\), we have \(\lambda=\frac{8\pm 1}{10}=\frac{9}{10},\frac{7}{10}.\) Since both are less than 1, both population decline, and perish.

For the populations to tend toward constant levels we need one of the eigenvalues to be \(\lambda=1\). (Like in #4.)

\[\begin{align*} 1&=\frac{8\pm\sqrt{16-30p}}{10}\\ 10&= 8\pm\sqrt{16-30p}\\ 2&=\pm\sqrt{16-30p}\\ 4&=16-30p\\ 30p&=12\\ p&=\frac{2}{5} \end{align*}\]

8.

There are eigenvalues both greater than 1 and less than 1, so this is a saddle point. The direction of greatest repulsion corresponds to the greatest eigenvalue: \(\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\-3\end{bmatrix}\). The direction of greatest attraction corresponds to the smallest eigenvalue: \(\begin{bmatrix}-3\\-3\\7\end{bmatrix}\)
